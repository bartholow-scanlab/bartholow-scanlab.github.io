JOIN THE RESEARCH TEAM

Undergraduate and postbac

  • We are continually seeking motivated students in psychology, neuroscience, biomedical engineering, computer science, and related fields to join the lab as undergraduate research assistants (RAs).
  • Research assistants work closely with our research team. Duties vary but typically include assisting with running laboratory sessions, collecting electroencephalogram (EEG) data (i.e., "brain waves"), and performing data entry and quality control.
  • Working in a psychology lab is a great way to learn what doing research is really like, to gain experience needed for applying to grad school or med school, and to learn unique skills that can be useful in studentsâ€™ future careers, whatever they might be.
  • Ideal candidates have a minimum GPA of 3.0 and are able to commit at least 10 hours per week. Because there is extensive training involved, preference will be given to research assistants who are able to commit to working at least two semesters. Students can either work in the lab for course credit (PSYC 2950 or 4950) or simply volunteer.
  • Complete the application below to be considered for an upcoming term (fall, spring, or summer) and we may contact you if there is an opening. Applications typically get reviewed monthly. Contact the Research Coordinator, Mr. Peck, for inquiries regarding RA applications.

CLAS Study

Recruitment Status: Recruiting

80%

The "CLAS Study" (CLAS is short for Characterizing Low Alcohol Sensitivity) is a five-year research study started in 2018 (see the news). This R01 grant is funded by the National Institute on Alcohol Abuse and Alcoholism (NIAAA R01AA025451). It involves recruitment of 420 emerging adults to participate in multiple lab sessions and rounds of daily assessments using a smartphone app (ecological momentary assessment). The overall goal of the study is to translate an influential theory of addiction from a preclinical (rodent-based) model to a human model, using a combination of psychophysiological, behavioral, subjective, and ecological assessments of alcohol cue-reactivity and drinking behavior. MU IRB Project #2010282
